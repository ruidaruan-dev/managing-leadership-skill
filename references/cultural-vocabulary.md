# Cultural Vocabulary (中国职场文化术语表)

A glossary of Chinese business and workplace culture terms essential for understanding and navigating leadership dynamics. Organized by category for quick reference.

---

## Core Relational Concepts (核心关系概念)

### 关系 (Guānxì) — Relationships/Connections
The foundational social infrastructure of Chinese business. Not merely "networking" but a persistent system of mutual obligations, trust, and reciprocity between specific individuals. Guanxi must be actively maintained through regular contact, favors, and social rituals. In workplace context: the quality of your guanxi with your leader often matters more than your job performance for career advancement.

### 面子 (Miànzi) — Face/Social Prestige
Social currency representing one's public reputation, status, and dignity. Can be given (给面子 — publicly supporting someone), saved (留面子 — helping someone avoid embarrassment), or lost (丢面子 — public humiliation or failure). In workplace context: causing your leader to lose face, even unintentionally, is one of the most damaging political mistakes a programmer can make.

### 脸 (Liǎn) — Moral Face/Integrity
Deeper than mianzi — represents one's moral character and integrity. Losing lian (丢脸) implies a fundamental character flaw. In workplace context: being caught lying, stealing credit, or betraying trust causes lian loss, which is far harder to recover from than mianzi loss.

### 人情 (Rénqíng) — Emotional Debt/Favors
The invisible ledger of social debts created by favors. Every act of help creates an obligation that must eventually be repaid. In workplace context: programmers build renqing by fixing critical bugs after hours, helping with technical problems outside their responsibility, or providing expertise that makes others look good.

### 圈子 (Quānzi) — Inner Circle
The tiered trust structure around every leader. Core circle members get information, protection, and opportunities first. In workplace context: career advancement often requires moving from the outer circle to at least the working circle of your leader.

### 中庸 (Zhōngyōng) — The Doctrine of the Mean
Traditional Chinese philosophical principle of seeking balance and avoiding extremes. In workplace context: being too visible attracts enemies; being too invisible gets you ignored. The optimal strategy is calibrated moderation.

## Organizational Terms (组织术语)

### 体制内 (Tǐzhì nèi) — Inside the System
Working within the state/public sector system (government, SOEs, public institutions). Implies: job security, rigid hierarchy, slower career progression, strong emphasis on political loyalty and process compliance.

### 体制外 (Tǐzhì wài) — Outside the System
Working in the private sector. Implies: higher risk/higher reward, more flexible hierarchy, faster career changes, emphasis on performance and market value.

### 编制 (Biānzhì) — Official Headcount/Establishment
The formally allocated positions within a state organization. Having a "编制" position means permanent employment status with full benefits. In workplace context: in SOEs, the distinction between 编制 and 合同工 (contract workers) creates a two-tier system with very different treatment.

### 级别 (Jíbié) — Administrative Rank/Level
The formal ranking system in Chinese organizations. In SOEs and government: follows a strict hierarchy (科级 → 处级 → 厅级 → 部级). In tech companies: follows technical levels (P5 → P6 → P7... or T1 → T2 → T3...).

### 科级/处级/厅级/部级 — SOE Administrative Ranks
科级 (Section level): First-level management, manages a small team.
处级 (Division level): Mid-level management, significant authority.
厅级 (Bureau level): Senior management, major organizational unit.
部级 (Ministry level): Top executive, head of ministry or equivalent.

### 汇报线 (Huìbào xiàn) — Reporting Line
The chain of who reports to whom. In matrix organizations, people may have multiple reporting lines (solid line vs. dotted line), creating ambiguity about authority and priorities.

## Communication Patterns (沟通模式)

### 打太极 (Dǎ tàijí) — Tai Chi / Deflection
Using indirect, evasive responses to avoid commitment or confrontation. When a leader "plays tai chi" with your request, they are neither approving nor rejecting — they are deferring indefinitely. In workplace context: recognize this as a soft "no" and either reframe your request or find another channel.

### 画饼 (Huà bǐng) — Drawing Cakes / Empty Promises
Making grand promises about future rewards (promotion, salary increase, equity) with no concrete timeline or commitment. In workplace context: when a leader "draws cakes" frequently, discount future promises and focus on extracting concrete, immediate value.

### 穿小鞋 (Chuān xiǎo xié) — Making Someone Wear Tight Shoes
Subtle retaliation through inconvenience: assigning undesirable tasks, withholding information, blocking opportunities, or giving negative reviews. It is never stated explicitly — the retaliation is always deniable. In workplace context: this is how leaders punish perceived slights without open conflict.

### 表态 (Biǎotài) — Making a Public Statement/Taking a Position
Being required to publicly declare support for a decision, initiative, or person. In Chinese organizations, failing to 表态 when expected is interpreted as opposition. In workplace context: in meetings where the leader announces a decision, be prepared to voice support — silence is not neutral.

### 站队 (Zhàn duì) — Choosing Sides
Aligning yourself with a specific faction or leader in organizational politics. Once you are perceived as belonging to a faction, your fate becomes linked to that faction's success or failure. In workplace context: avoid premature faction alignment; build relationships across multiple groups when possible.

### 打招呼 (Dǎ zhāohu) — Making a Greeting/Informal Request
An informal, often verbal, request that bypasses official channels. "某领导打了招呼" means a leader has informally requested something be done without formal documentation. In workplace context: these requests are socially binding despite having no paper trail.

## Hierarchy Terms (层级称谓)

### 领导 (Lǐngdǎo) — Leader
Generic term for anyone in a position of authority above you. Can refer to your direct manager or any senior person. Using "领导" when addressing someone implies respect for their position.

### 老板 (Lǎobǎn) — Boss
More informal than 领导. In private companies, usually refers to the company owner/founder. In casual usage, can refer to any direct supervisor.

### 上级 (Shàngjí) — Superior
Formal term for anyone higher in the organizational hierarchy. Used in written communication and formal contexts.

### 一把手 (Yī bǎ shǒu) — Number One / Top Leader
The highest-ranking person in an organization or department. The one who makes final decisions. "一把手说了算" means "the number one has the final say."

### 分管领导 (Fēn guǎn lǐngdǎo) — Supervising Leader
The specific senior leader who oversees your functional area. You may report to a direct manager but the 分管领导 has broader authority over your department's direction and resources.

### 直属领导 (Zhíshǔ lǐngdǎo) — Direct Supervisor
Your immediate reporting manager. The person who signs your performance review and manages your day-to-day work.

## Political Dynamics (政治动态)

### 向上管理 (Xiàng shàng guǎnlǐ) — Managing Up
The practice of proactively managing your relationship with your boss rather than passively waiting for direction. Includes: understanding their priorities, adapting to their communication style, making them look good, and shaping their perception of your value.

### 内卷 (Nèi juǎn) — Involution
Intense, often irrational competition that leads to escalating effort without proportional returns. In workplace context: when everyone works 12-hour days to produce the same output that 8-hour days would achieve, because no one dares be the first to leave.

### 996 — Working 9am to 9pm, 6 Days a Week
The infamous tech industry work culture. Technically illegal but widespread. Used as both a literal schedule and a metaphor for overwork culture.

### 35岁危机 (35 Suì Wēijī) — The Age-35 Crisis
The widespread belief (and often reality) in Chinese tech that programmers become unemployable after age 35. Companies prefer younger, cheaper workers. This creates career anxiety that shapes much of the programmer's strategic thinking about management track vs. IC track.

### 背锅 (Bēi guō) — Carrying the Blame Pot
Taking the blame for a failure, especially one you did not cause. In Chinese organizations, blame flows downward. In workplace context: build documentation and paper trails to protect against blame assignment. When blame is unavoidable, negotiate for it to be framed as a "learning experience" rather than a personal failure.

### 甩锅 (Shuǎi guō) — Throwing the Blame Pot
Deflecting blame onto someone else. The opposite of 背锅. In workplace context: recognize when someone is setting you up to 背锅 (take blame) and create documentation that distributes responsibility appropriately.

## Tech Industry Slang (互联网行业黑话)

### 卷 (Juǎn) — To Involute / Grind
Verb form of 内卷. "他太卷了" means "he grinds too hard" — working excessive hours that creates pressure for everyone else.

### 躺平 (Tǎng píng) — Lying Flat
The opposite of 卷 — doing the minimum required work, refusing to participate in the involution arms race. A philosophical stance against unsustainable work culture. In workplace context: a risky strategy that works only if you have sufficient irreplaceability.

### 摸鱼 (Mō yú) — Catching Fish / Slacking Off
Working minimally while appearing busy. The art of productive rest during work hours. In workplace context: universal but must be done strategically — appearing busy to the right people at the right times.

### 跑路 (Pǎo lù) — Running Away / Quitting
Leaving a company, often suddenly. "他跑路了" means "he quit and left." Can imply escaping a bad situation.

### 毕业 (Bì yè) — Graduating / Being Laid Off
Tech industry euphemism for being laid off. "从XX公司毕业了" means "got laid off from XX company." The euphemism softens the stigma.

### 对齐 (Duì qí) — Alignment
Originally means "align" in typographic terms. Now corporate speak for "getting on the same page." "我们先对齐一下" means "let's align first." Overuse is a meme in tech circles.

### 赋能 (Fù néng) — Empowerment
Corporate buzzword meaning to enable or empower. "AI赋能业务" means "AI empowering business." Often used in presentations with minimal substance behind it. Useful to know because leaders who use this term heavily are often more presentation-oriented than execution-oriented.

### 抓手 (Zhuā shǒu) — Handle/Lever
Corporate speak for a key initiative or approach. "这个项目是我们数字化转型的重要抓手" — "This project is a key lever for our digital transformation." Useful for framing your work in leader-legible language.

### 颗粒度 (Kē lì dù) — Granularity
The level of detail in a plan or report. "颗粒度要再细一些" — "The granularity needs to be finer." When a leader asks for "更细的颗粒度," they want more detail in your reports or plans.
